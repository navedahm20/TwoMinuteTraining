Explaining Behavioral Engineering

Ultimately our goal as trainers - I mean, problem solvers - is to change behavior (specifically that behavior which will improve performance). How do we do that? The process that this presenter suggested is very similar to the one discussed a couple posts ago. Take a look:

Just like in the previous model, we must first define the business goals. This allows us to identify the behavior change which, in turn, determines the learning solution. Implementing the learning solution induces a behavior change, which impacts the business goal.

When we're developing a learning solution, there are 3 questions we must ask about the learner:

  1. Do they have the knowledge to change their behavior?
  2. Do they have the opportunity to change their behavior?
  3. Do they have the motivation to change their behavior?

If the answer to the first question is no, that can potentially be addressed by a learning solution. The second and third questions cannot be addressed by a learning solution (not directly, at least), but they're just as important to consider. Even if you develop an effective learning solution (that gives learners the knowledge to change their behavior), if they don't have the opportunity or motivation to change, you will not see any behavior change.

Let's consdier a simple example. Let's say you have a team of call center representatives. When they get a complaint from a client, they're supposed to transfer the call to your client relations team, but what you're observing is that they are transferring calls to the sales team. So, you develop this amazing training course to teach them how to properly transfer the calls to client relations. You assess the learners and find that they all know how to do this process correctly. However, you notice that they're still transferring calls to the sales team. Now, when you begin to consider opportunity and motivation, you dig a little deeper and learn that your call center representatives are financially rewarded for every call that they transfer to the sales team. In this example, your call center representatives lack the motivation to transfer calls to anybody except the sales team. Now, this is a very simplified example, but it's just to illustrate the importance of considering opportuinty and motivation in addition to knowledge, when you're developing a learning solution.
